He Who Saw the Deep

The Epic of Gilgamesh: Tablet I

(Click to begin)

He who journeyed beyond all worlds,
who saw the deep and knew all secrets—

He who remembered the time before the Flood,
and brought wisdom from distant lands.

He traveled beyond the edges of the earth,
and carved his story in stone for all to see.

He built Uruk, crowned with shining walls,
and raised the temple of Eanna for the goddess Ishtar.

The Seven Sages laid the city's foundations,
each brick fired in the ancient kilns of time.

In a copper-bound box he hid his tale,
waiting for those who would open its bronze clasp.

Two-thirds divine, one-third mortal—
crafted by the gods in glory.

Supreme among kings,
breaker of walls.

Who else could stand and say:
"I am King"?

His name echoes through the hollow stones,
sung by generations who remember.

He roamed the walls of Uruk,
head held high, fierce as a wild bull.

No rival dared to raise a weapon.
His commands shook the streets.

No son was left to his father.
No daughter was safe in her mother’s arms.

Bold, eminent, wise—
yet he tore the joy from their homes.

The gods heard the people's cries,
rising like smoke toward the heavens.

The gods cried out to Aruru:
"Shape a rival to the stormy heart of Gilgamesh!"

From the clay of the wilderness
and the silence between stars,
Aruru shaped a being of might and wildness.
Hair covered his body like the fields of Ashnan,
his locks flowed like ripened grain.

He knew neither city nor the speech of men,
but lived with gazelles upon the open plain,
drank with the beasts at the watering hole.
His body, mighty and unshorn,
his spirit born of the gods of strength.

For three days the trapper saw him,
and each day fear seized his heart.

"Father!" he cried, "There roams a mighty one,
strong as the stone from the heavens. He fills in my pits, tears out my traps,
and frees the beasts from my grasp!"

"Go, my son," the father said,
"To Uruk, where Gilgamesh rules the world of men."

"Tell him of the wild one who roams the mountains,
strong as the stone of heaven itself."

Gilgamesh will send the sacred harlot, Shamhat,
whose beauty is a force no beast can withstand.

"When the animals drink at the water’s edge,
let her cast off her robe,
let her reveal the mystery of her body,
and he will be drawn from the wild forever."

The trapper raced to Uruk,
and stood trembling before mighty Gilgamesh.

"There is a being of the mountains," he cried,
"Strong as a meteor fallen from Anu's hand!"

Gilgamesh answered:
"Go. Bring Shamhat.
At the watering place, she will unveil herself,
and the wild man will be caught by her gaze."

So the trapper and Shamhat set out,
walking for three days into the silent wilds.

At the watering hole they waited,
as the beasts came to slake their thirst.

And with them came Enkidu,
child of the mountains,
who ate with gazelles
and drank where the lions drank.

Then Shamhat beheld him —
wild, untamed, born of the wilderness.

"Unclasp your robe," they said,
"Let him see your beauty,
draw him from the beasts to you."

She bared her body without fear,
and Enkidu, drawn by her scent and sight,
forgot the wild and came into her arms.

For six days and seven nights,
Enkidu lay with her, and did not rise.
His hunger met her mystery,
until he was emptied of the wild within.

He sat at her feet,
and the wildness drained from his body.
"You are no longer of the beasts," she whispered.
"You walk the path of men now."
"Come with me to Uruk,
where song fills the streets
and the faces of men shine bright."
Enkidu bowed his head.
The plains no longer called to him.
He longed for a name, and a place among men.
